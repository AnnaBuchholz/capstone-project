Timeline

1500s by Century

1557 - The plus sign was created. (It is derived from the latin word "et" which means "and".) Robert Recorde's book The Whetstone of Witte (written in 1557) was the first time the plus and minus symbols were introduced to the English./p>

1557 - The minus sign was created. Just like the plus and equals sign, the minus sign was introduced in Robert Recorde's book The Whetstone of Witte./p>

1557 - This was the first time the = symbol was used as shorthand for "is equal to." This symbol was chosen because "what is more equal than two parallel lines of equal length?". Similarly to the plus and minus signs, this symbol was also first used in the Whetstone of Witte by Robert Recorde.

 1600s by Century

1618 - One of the earliest times the multiplication symbol (x) was used. It was found in the anonymous appendix in Edward Wright's translation of John Napier's "A Description of the Admirable Table of Logarithmes Later on, since it could be confused for a regular "x" in English, Leibniz began to use the dot product Insert dot symbol here symbol instead.

1642 - Isaac Newton was born on December 25 of 1642. He died on March 20, 1727.

1646 - Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz was born on July 1 of 1646. He died in 1716 on November 14.

1659 - This is when the obelus (yes, the different division signs have names and the "obelus" is the insert obelus here sign) was first used. It was found in Jahann Rahn's algebra book Teutsche Algebra written in 1659. The solidus (this symbol "/") was introduced by De Morgan in 1845. In 1684 Leibniz introduced the colon as a symbol for division and ratios in his Acta Eruditorum.

 1700s by Century

1716 - Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz died on November 14 of 1646 at 70 years old. He was born on July 1, 1646.

1727 - Isaac Newton died on March 20 of 1727 at 84 years old. He was born on December 25, 1642.

 1800s by Century

1859 - Bernhard Riemann proposed the famous Riemann Hypothesis which remains unsolved to this day. It is one of the seven Millenial Problems.

 1900s by Century

1993 - Fermat's Last Theorem, first proposed in 1637, was finally proven correct by mathematician Andrew Wiles in a 729 page proof written over a period of seven years.
